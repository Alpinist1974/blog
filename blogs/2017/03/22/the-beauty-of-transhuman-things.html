The Beauty of Transhuman Things

by Matt

March 22, 2017
in Blogs

I was flipping through my Robinson Jeffers anthology yesterday evening before bed, reading one of his last poems, “Granddaughter” when I was struck by a certain turn of phrase, as I often am with his poetry:

When she is eighteen / I’ll not be here. I hope she will find her natural elements, / Laughter and violence; and in her quiet times / The beauty of things - the beauty of transhuman things, / Without which we are all lost.

It was that one line - the beauty of things - the beauty of transhuman things - that really grabbed me. You see, the term transhuman in these times is far more likely to suggest a notion of technologically enhanced human consciousness than what Jeffers had intended, which is even going in the opposite direction. Transhumanism posits that consciousness will one day defy the mortal limits of our biology and usher in the singularity, an event in which human consciousness essentially becomes data and merges with the artificial intelligence currently evolving out of our most advanced technologies.

Transhumanism as a term approaching the sense in which we understand it today as advanced by Ray Kurzweil and company, was first popularized in a 1957 essay by the biologist Julian Huxley, brother of Aldous and grandson of Thomas Henry Huxley, (“Darwin’s Bulldog”). But Jeffers’ use of the term transhuman precedes Huxley’s by many years, and is diametrically opposed, in many ways, to our current understanding of the expression “transhuman.”

Jeffers devoted most of his work to developing what one might call a poetics of the inhuman – An aesthetic theory and philosophy in which human history and human consciousness is not the sun around which everything else in nature revolves, but instead perhaps a distant moon, orbiting a planet defined by geologic timescales and natural forces beyond the grasp of any merely intellectual human understanding. These symbols of the inhuman are represented in his poetry by the ubiquitous granite that defines the Carmel coastline as well as the house and tower he built from granite and spent much of his life inhabiting; Or the sea itself, as it breaks down all things over time and represents the ur-womb from which terrestrial life emerged.
